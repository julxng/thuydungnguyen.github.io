Sonic Exploration

Part of "Resonance of the Unseen" - Bard College Senior Project, December 2023

Overview

How can we rediscover mindful presence through the sounds of everyday life? In a world saturated with sensory input, we often fail to truly listen. Sonic Exploration is an interactive sound installation that simplifies life's rhythms into identifiable sounds, challenging visitors to actively engage with the acoustic environment around them.

The installation features 20 speakers arranged in space, each playing a distinct sound from nature, humanity, and our surroundings. Visitors must identify and locate each sound source, transforming passive listening into an active, playful puzzle. This sonic odyssey transcends mere listening—it becomes a vehicle for memory, a shared experience connecting the individual to the universal.

Technical Implementation

Hardware: Arduino Mega 2560, 20 small speakers, 20 LED-lit arcade buttons, 2 large LED control buttons (Start/Stop), 2 10-channel amplifiers, 2 12-channel audio interfaces, screw terminal breakout shield, 20 PVC pipe elbows and flanges for speaker housing

Software: Arduino for button input and LED control, MaxMSP for multi-channel audio playback and routing, serial communication protocol between Arduino and MaxMSP

Space: 9.84 × 16.4 ft installation room with speakers distributed throughout, creating an immersive 360-degree sound field

Interaction Design:

  1. Press GREEN button to START
  2. Listen carefully and press the button below the speaker that's playing
  3. After correctly identifying all 20 speakers, an automatic sonic composition plays
  4. Incorrect button presses trigger feedback sound; press GREEN to restart
  5. Press YELLOW button to STOP and reset

Concept & Artistic Context

Sonic Exploration is part of "Resonance of the Unseen", my Bard College senior exhibition exploring sensory perception through sound, scent, and touch. The exhibition confronts the sensory saturation of contemporary life by elevating the unobserved aspects of perception, inviting a rediscovery of elemental experiences.

The auditory installation challenges visitors to pinpoint sounds in space, emphasizing the importance of mindful presence. Each sound—birdsong, footsteps, ocean waves, breathing—represents a thread in the tapestry of life. By requiring active identification rather than passive consumption, the work reframes listening as an act of discovery.

The piece draws inspiration from sound artists like Janet Cardiff (spatial audio walks) and Ryoji Ikeda (data sonification), while incorporating game-like interaction to make the experience playful and accessible.

Development Process

Technical Challenges: Coordinating 20 independent audio channels while maintaining Arduino-MaxMSP communication reliability. Initial prototypes had timing issues where button presses didn't sync with audio playback. Solved by implementing a robust serial communication protocol with error handling and LED feedback states.

Sound Curation: Selecting 20 sounds that were distinctive yet familiar required extensive testing. Sounds needed to be recognizable but not obvious—inviting curiosity without frustration. Categories include natural sounds (rain, wind, birds), human sounds (footsteps, breathing, laughter), and environmental sounds (traffic, doors, water).

Spatial Design: Speaker placement created a three-dimensional sound field that required visitors to move through space. This physical navigation paralleled the cognitive navigation of identifying sounds, making the experience fully embodied rather than just auditory.

Exhibition & Response

Exhibited December 2-16, 2023 at Fisher Studio Arts Building, Bard College. The installation could be experienced alone or collaboratively, with groups often working together to identify sounds, creating spontaneous social bonding through shared listening.

Visitors reported heightened awareness of everyday sounds after experiencing the installation—noticing acoustic details they'd previously overlooked. The game-like structure made the meditative act of deep listening accessible and engaging, removing the intimidation often associated with experimental sound art.

Outcome & Reflection

Sonic Exploration successfully transformed listening from passive reception to active discovery. The installation demonstrated how technology can facilitate mindfulness rather than distract from it— using Arduino and MaxMSP not to create novel sounds, but to heighten awareness of sounds we've always heard but rarely noticed.

Next Directions: Exploring adaptive difficulty (sounds become more subtle as visitors improve), location-specific sound libraries (site-specific versions using local acoustic environments), and networked versions where multiple installations communicate, creating a distributed sonic experience across locations.
